Drought & Conservation: Exploring the Relationship Between Drought
Exposure and Conservation Program Enrollment
Reid Hensen
INTRODUCTION
Drought can have significant implications for the wellbeing of livestock
producers. It can force grassland producers to sell their herds,
dramatically impact profits, and create market instability (Countryman et
al. 2016). In addition to the variety of insurance programs that exist,
producers may also seek to use working lands conservation programs
(WLCPs) to mitigate drought risk (Wallander et al. 2013). The present
project looks at the USDA Environmental Quality Incentives Program
(EQIP) program to see if producers are (in aggregate) using the program
as a tool in for drought management. This program is the highest funded
WLCP in the Farm Bill programs and 60% of total funding is mandated to
go towards grazing based practices. The below graph demonstrates EQIP
funding when compared to other WLCPs.
Results indicate that while there may be a statistically
significant effect in some of the lag years,
the effect size is small. Overall, we see that producers
may not see EQIP as a drought mitigation tool but are
employing practices for other benefits
• This project aims to provide insight into how
conservation dollars are being spent and the
implications of that spending
• Drought mitigation is unlikely to be a major cause of
EQIP grazing enrollments
• If NRCS wishes to aid in drought management
strategies for producers, they likely will need to
create new practices
• Future work:
• Explore specific practices that are implemented
after major drought
• Explore national dataset with precipitation and
temperature as a substitute for the vegetation
control variable
